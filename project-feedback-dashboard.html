🧩 Background

review work is often hard to see

In regulated environments, document review is critical but often opaque. Deviation, CAPA, and related records may pass through multiple reviewers, yet visibility into feedback burden, timing, and revision-loop formation is limited.

As review cycles lengthen, teams often know that a document is "still under review," but not what type of comments are driving the delay, where bottlenecks are forming, or whether a loop is escalating across rounds.

The core problem is not only delay itself, but the lack of a shared operational view of that delay.

⚠️ Observation

friction without visibility

Observed pain points

  • Repeated revision loops were difficult to observe as patterns rather than isolated events.
  • Feedback burden was uneven across reviewers, but the imbalance was hard to see clearly.
  • Small wording or formatting comments could still generate multi-day back-and-forth.
  • The review process often felt slow, but the source of friction remained unclear.

Why this mattered

These were not simply communication issues. They reflected a missing observability layer in the review workflow — one that could make hidden friction visible before delays accumulated further.

💬 In structured review systems, delay is not only a timing problem — it is also a visibility problem.

🎯 Design question

HCI framing

How can review workflows be visualized in a way that helps users notice bottlenecks, comment patterns, and revision burden earlier — without automating judgment or altering validated systems?

↔ System change illustration

before / after

Before

  • Review status was often reduced to simple "in progress" updates.
  • Delay patterns remained distributed across rounds, emails, and comments.
  • Friction sources were interpreted informally and often from memory.
  • Repeated loop formation could be felt, but not easily visualized.
  • Comment burden and reviewer asymmetry were difficult to compare.

After

  • Review signals were surfaced through summaries, heatmaps, and workflow traces.
  • Loop-heavy patterns became visible as structured workflow behavior.
  • Checklist and classifier support helped differentiate likely comment types.
  • Simulation added a lightweight reflection layer before feedback escalated.
  • No validated templates were modified in the process.
This project reframes review delay as an observability problem, not only a coordination problem.

🧠 Prototype concept

visibility + interpretation + reflection

1. Overview & heatmaps

Surface category frequency, reviewer turnaround, bottleneck concentration, and revision density.

2. Comment support

Use a lightweight rule-based classifier and checklist to distinguish likely comment types and reduce unnecessary loop inflation.

3. Simulation layer

Let users explore how reviewer role, comment framing, and round number may affect expected friction.

🧪 Method

synthetic data + prototype evaluation

1. Dataset construction

Created a synthetic review-workflow dataset (100 records) representing comment categories, review rounds, severity, turnaround time, reopening, reviewer role, and document type.

2. Prototype implementation

Built an interactive dashboard in Python/Streamlit with workflow summaries, revision heatmaps, a checklist assistant, a rule-based comment classifier, document-level journey tracing, and reviewer simulation.

3. Computational extension

Added lightweight logic for comment-type classification, pattern-based friction estimation, and reviewer-profile simulation to support reflection before feedback submission.

4. Lightweight usability feedback

Conducted an informal prototype feedback session with six users to assess clarity, interpretability, interface organization, and perceived usefulness in review-workflow contexts.

👥 Lightweight usability feedback

n = 6

Testing setup

Because validated systems cannot freely adopt unvalidated interface changes, the prototype was evaluated outside the live system. Participants were asked to explore the dashboard and complete lightweight review tasks using it as a simulated workflow-support layer.

prototype artifact exploratory session n = 6

What was measured

  • purpose clarity and overall interpretability
  • visibility of friction and revision patterns
  • clarity of checklist and classifier features
  • ease of using the reviewer simulation
  • perceived real-world usefulness

Quantitative summary

A short usability survey was completed by six participants. All reported prior experience with document review, QA/QC, or regulated documentation. The prototype received high ratings across all dimensions: purpose clarity (4.83/5), friction visibility (4.83/5), checklist clarity (5.0/5), simulation clarity (4.83/5), and real-world usefulness (100%).

purpose clarity: 4.83 / 5 friction visibility: 4.83 / 5 checklist clarity: 5.0 / 5 simulation clarity: 4.83 / 5 real-world usefulness: 100%

Key interpretation

Users did not mainly struggle with the interface itself. One recurring insight was that the workflow-friction concept initially felt unfamiliar — suggesting the need for brief conceptual onboarding when introducing the prototype to first-time users. This points toward adoption barriers being conceptual rather than interactional. Most useful features were Friction Heatmap (33.3%), Reviewer Simulation (33.3%), Checklist Assistant / Comment Classifier (16.7%), and Document Journey (16.7%).

Why the result matters

The main contribution is not simply efficiency. It is the transformation of hidden review work into interpretable workflow signals. The prototype supports procedural visibility and coordination without trying to automate reviewer judgment.

📄 Project extension

portfolio direction

This project currently functions as a portfolio-facing HCI case study rather than a finalized paper. Its main contribution is to show how review friction, comment burden, and revision loops can be made visible through a lightweight computational support prototype.

The broader design implication is that workflow dashboards in regulated environments can function as coordination support — not just reporting tools. They can help teams interpret procedural conditions earlier without intervening in validated documentation systems themselves.

💬 Reflection

why it is HCI

This project reframed review delay as both a workflow problem and an information-design problem. What teams often experience as "slow review" is not only latency — it is also a failure of visibility, categorization, and shared interpretation.

The prototype therefore acts less like an automation system and more like a human-centered support layer: helping users notice, interpret, and respond to procedural friction before it expands into repeated loops.

Importantly, the feedback session also suggested that the main challenge was not interface complexity itself, but initial unfamiliarity with the workflow-friction framing. That insight pushed the project from "dashboard UI" toward a broader HCI question about how new procedural concepts become legible to users.
