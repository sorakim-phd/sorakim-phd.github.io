🧩 Background

closure pressure in GMP investigation

In GMP environments, deviation investigations must be formally documented within defined timeframes. Quality teams operate under consistent pressure to close records — not because investigations are necessarily complete, but because open deviations create regulatory exposure and workflow bottlenecks.

This creates what can be called closure pressure: a structural condition in which the timeline for committing to an explanation is set by documentation requirements rather than by the state of the investigator's reasoning. The result is that investigators may be documenting a root cause before they have had sufficient time to consider alternatives.

If this is true, it has downstream consequences: CAPAs designed around the first plausible explanation may not address the full causal picture, and responsibility may be assigned before systemic contributors have been examined.

💬 In actual GMP practice, most investigators spend far less than 10 minutes on initial causal analysis. Not because they are careless — but because the system demands closure before reasoning has time to develop.

⚠️ Observation

field-level pattern

What was observed in practice

  • Investigators typically committed to a root cause explanation early in the process.
  • Time pressure was consistently cited as the primary driver of early closure — not confidence in the explanation.
  • Systemic and procedural contributors were less likely to surface under time pressure.
  • Investigation records often reflected the first plausible explanation rather than the most complete one.

Why this is a design problem

Premature causal convergence is not a training failure or an individual competence issue. It is a structural consequence of how investigation workflows are designed — the timeline for documentation closure does not align with the timeline for reasoning development.

This reframes the problem as an HCI question: how can investigation workflows be designed to protect reasoning space without compromising documentation requirements?

🎯 Research question

empirical framing

How does causal reasoning about a GMP deviation develop over a 10-minute unassisted thinking period — and does the observed convergence pattern suggest that early documentation closure produces systematically incomplete causal explanations?

🧠 Method

paper-based · time-point observation · N = 5

The study used a structured paper-based reasoning task with no digital tool or scaffold. This was a deliberate methodological choice: using a scaffold would confound the observation, making it impossible to separate natural reasoning development from tool-assisted reasoning. The goal was to observe what unassisted causal reasoning looks like under a naturalistic time constraint.

Scenario

An ELISA assay result exceeded its specification limit — a common GMP deviation type. Participants were asked to identify possible causes freely, without being directed toward any particular explanation category.

Recording template — 4 steps

Step 1: initial cause written immediately. Step 2: additional causes added during the 5–10 minute thinking period. Step 3: connections between causes described. Step 4: explicit comparison of initial vs. revised explanation — what changed and why.

Post-task questions

Participants reported whether their explanation changed, whether new causes were added, and when their thinking shifted — at the start, in the middle, or toward the end of the 10 minutes.

Why no prototype

The paper format preserved the naturalistic reasoning condition. A digital tool would have introduced structure — and the research question was specifically about what reasoning looks like without it. That baseline observation is what motivates the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder as a design response.

🧪 Empirical study

N = 5 · GMP-experienced · time-based observation

🔑 Key finding: uniform initial convergence, consistent revision

Across five participants, initial causal reasoning was uniformly singular: all participants proposed only one likely cause at the outset, although the content of that cause varied across method-, equipment-, and human-related explanations. This suggests that the key issue is not simply blame attribution, but early causal convergence itself.

Most participants added at least one additional cause. Some began connecting causes, noting dependencies and contributing conditions.
10 min — revised
5 / 5 revised
All participants changed or expanded their initial explanation. Several reprioritised — moving procedural or systemic causes above their original explanation.

Cause type diversity

Initial causes varied across participants: assay method error, equipment malfunction, environmental contamination, reagent quality issue, and operator action. Despite this content diversity, the structural pattern was identical — single-hypothesis initial framing, followed by expansion over time.

Revision direction

When participants revised, they consistently moved toward more systemic framings. One participant who began with "operator error" later added "the SOP didn't make this step clear enough." This shift — from individual to system — only emerged with time, not at the outset.

The closure pressure implication

In actual GMP investigation practice, most investigators spend far less than 10 minutes on initial causal analysis before documentation begins — not because of carelessness, but because closure timelines demand it. This study suggests that the explanations being formally documented may reflect reasoning that has not yet had time to fully develop. The problem is structural: investigation design does not currently protect the reasoning time that this data suggests is needed.

What this means for investigation design

If reasoning continues to develop meaningfully within a 10-minute window, and documentation typically begins before that window closes, then investigation records may systematically underrepresent causal complexity. Closure pressure — the structural condition that drives early documentation — may be producing premature causal convergence as a predictable output of the current workflow design. This is not an argument against timeliness. It is an argument for designing investigation workflows that protect reasoning space within those timelines.

🔗 Connection to the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder

observation → design response

This pilot study establishes the problem that the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder is designed to address. If premature causal convergence is a structural condition — produced by closure pressure, not individual failure — then the design response should be structural too: a scaffold that protects reasoning space and keeps alternative hypotheses visible before closure.

This study — the problem

Without structure or time protection, investigators converge on a single cause immediately. The content varies — but the structure does not. Early causal convergence is the norm, not the exception, under real GMP closure pressure.

RCA Scaffolder — the response

The scaffolder was designed to do what this study shows unassisted reasoning cannot reliably do under closure pressure: keep multiple hypotheses active, surface alternative causal pathways, and resist premature accountability convergence before the investigation narrative is fixed.

This study shows what reasoning looks like before a scaffold exists. The RCA Reasoning Scaffolder shows what it looks like when structural support is provided.

📄 Paper in progress

research output

This pilot study is being developed into a paper on premature causal convergence, closure pressure, and the design of investigation workflows in regulated environments. The paper contributes a behavioral observation to the broader research program: early-stage causal reasoning in GMP investigation is structurally convergent, and the conditions that produce that convergence are design-addressable.

The paper also contributes methodologically: using time-point observation of unassisted reasoning to study investigation behavior without requiring deployment inside validated systems.

💬 Reflection

why it matters

This project started from a practical observation: nobody spends more than 10 minutes thinking about a deviation before they have to start writing. That observation — which felt like a limitation of the work environment — turned out to be the research finding itself.

From an HCI perspective, the contribution is to reframe a familiar quality problem — incomplete root cause analysis — as a design problem rather than a training or competence problem. The reasoning capacity is there. What is missing is the structural protection that would allow it to develop before closure pressure forces a commitment.

That is what connects this study to the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder, and what anchors both projects in the broader research argument: in regulated environments, the design of investigation workflows shapes not only what gets recorded, but what gets thought.
