about / researcher profile / human-centered systems

I study human-centered systems for regulated work.

My work focuses on documentation, procedural cognition, workflow visibility, and decision support in high-stakes environments. I am especially interested in how people interpret dense procedural systems and how support layers can make that work more navigable without collapsing ambiguity too early.

Research identity. HCI for complex procedural environments — especially documents, investigations, interpretation, and the design of systems that support careful human work.
HCI regulatory UX procedural cognition decision support

Designing for judgment under constraint.

I care about systems where people must interpret, document, justify, and decide under formal requirements. In those environments, usability is not cosmetic — it shapes the quality of work itself.

documents as interfaces workflow friction careful work

Why this feels like HCI.

I am interested in systems that shape how people read, interpret, coordinate, and act. In regulated environments, even documents and review structures function like interfaces for judgment.

real-world workflows high-stakes systems organizational life

Grounded in actual regulated work.

My questions come out of pharmaceutical quality systems, where SOPs, deviations, CAPA, RCA, verification work, and audit-facing documentation are part of everyday practice.

what I study

Documents, workflows, and judgment

I study how people navigate dense procedural systems: how documents distribute interpretive burden, how workflows create friction, and how support tools can help people reason more clearly without turning complex work into something falsely simple.

research approach

I treat regulated environments as HCI problems.

Rather than seeing procedures and documentation as passive background materials, I treat them as active structures that shape navigation, timing, interpretation, and judgment.

That means moving between design questions and conceptual questions: how systems can become more usable in practice, and how documents, investigations, and support tools shape what becomes visible, legible, and actionable in organizational life.

human-centered systems → procedural cognition → workflow visibility → decision support
how I work

Close to real work, not abstracted away from it.

I like research that stays close to actual practice. Methodologically, I combine document analysis, structured qualitative reading, field-based observation, and lightweight computational analysis using tools such as Excel and Python.

Across projects, I keep returning to one underlying question: how do we make high-stakes procedural systems more legible for the people inside them?

What my work is trying to do

HCI × regulated environments

Across projects and papers, I am trying to make procedural work more legible, more navigable, and more supportive of careful judgment — especially in environments where the source systems themselves cannot be easily redesigned.

01

Make friction visible

I study where documentation, workflow order, and interface structure create unnecessary interpretive burden.

02

Support judgment, not replace it

I care about tools and support layers that help people think more clearly without pretending complex work can be fully automated away.

03

Keep accountability in view

I want systems that make reasoning, interpretation, and responsibility more visible rather than prematurely compressed or hidden.
