About

Why responsibility became my HCI question.

I spent more than four years in pharmaceutical quality control working inside GMP systems — and watching colleagues struggle inside them too. Over time, the central question stopped being compliance alone. It became a question about responsibility: when work is pressured, procedural, and heavily documented, how do systems shape what people can see, justify, and ultimately be held responsible for?

Research identity

From QC practice to HCI research.

That question came directly from practice. I am not arguing that regulation should disappear, or that formal requirements are the problem by themselves. Regulated systems matter. My research asks something narrower and more practical: how do documents, workflows, and tools produce the conditions under which judgment and responsibility are assigned inside regulated work?

HCI regulatory UX procedural cognition decision support GMP environments
Why this feels like HCI
Documents as interfaces for judgment.

My starting point was everyday QC work — SOPs, deviation investigations, CAPAs, root cause analysis, review cycles, and the constant need to document decisions in ways that would still make sense later.

Grounding
Four years in actual regulated work.

In that setting, the biggest issue often was not only what the rule said, but how accountability moved through documents, workflows, and investigation structures — and how quickly a complex situation could narrow to a person, team, or single explanation.

Orientation
HCI at the center, STS as grounding.

That practical question first pushed me toward STS writing. The conceptual papers ask how accountability gets organized, documented, and narrowed inside formal systems before anyone is judged as having failed.

What I study
Documents, workflows, and judgment

The six HCI projects grew out of that same concern. I am not trying to weaken regulation. I am asking how everyday systems organize judgment and responsibility inside regulation — and how those conditions can be redesigned without bypassing formal constraints or replacing human judgment.

Research approach
I treat regulated environments as HCI problems.

So I build external, non-invasive prototypes: retrieval layers, navigation tools, reasoning scaffolds, and visibility systems that surface burden earlier and make difficult work easier to navigate.

The HCI side of my work asks what happens when documentation is hard to retrieve, cross-references are hard to follow, review work is hard to see, or investigation reasoning closes too early.

human-centered systems → procedural cognition → workflow visibility → decision support
How I work
Close to real work, not abstracted away from it.

The STS side asks how those same conditions shape accountability: what becomes visible as evidence, which explanations seem easiest to document and justify, and who ultimately carries the burden.

Taken together, the program moves from lived QC experience to theory and back again — from workplace difficulty as an empirical problem to design as a way of improving the conditions under which judgments are made.

At a glance
Research snapshot

My goal is not automated decision-making or softer standards. It is stronger conditions for human reasoning, navigation, and accountability in high-stakes systems where mistakes carry real consequences.

Program at a glance
Three recurring mechanisms organize the work.
01. navigation burden in dense document systems
02. premature closure in investigative reasoning
03. accountability visibility gaps in review workflows
Across projects, I study how these conditions are produced by system design — and how support layers can improve reasoning without weakening regulation or replacing human judgment.
Research style
What my work is trying to do
01
Make friction visible

Methodologically, I combine document analysis, structured qualitative observation, usability testing, and lightweight computational prototyping in Python and Streamlit. The prototypes are built as external layers so they can study and support regulated work without modifying validated systems.

02
Support judgment, not replace it

Intellectually, I work between STS and HCI: STS helps me analyze how accountability is organized in formal systems; HCI lets me test how interfaces, workflows, and support layers might improve working conditions on the ground.

03
Keep accountability in view

Across the portfolio, the through-line is simple: regulation stays, but the people inside regulated systems deserve environments that are easier to navigate and reason within.
