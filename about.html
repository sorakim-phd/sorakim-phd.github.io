about

I study human-centered systems for regulated work.

My work focuses on documentation, procedural cognition, workflow visibility, and decision support in high-stakes environments — especially how people interpret dense procedural systems and how support layers can make that work more navigable without collapsing ambiguity too early.

HCI regulatory UX procedural cognition decision support GMP environments
// research identity
Designing for judgment under constraint.

I care about systems where people must interpret, document, justify, and decide under formal requirements. In those environments, usability is not cosmetic — it shapes the quality of work itself.

// why this feels like HCI
Documents as interfaces for judgment.

In regulated environments, even procedural documents and review structures function like interfaces. They shape how people read, interpret, coordinate, and act.

// grounding
Four years in actual regulated work.

My questions come out of pharmaceutical quality control — where SOPs, deviation reports, CAPAs, root cause analysis, and audit-facing documentation are part of everyday practice.

// what I study
Documents, workflows, and judgment

I study how people navigate dense procedural systems: how documents distribute interpretive burden, how workflows create friction, and how support tools can help people reason more clearly without turning complex work into something falsely simple.

// research approach
I treat regulated environments as HCI problems.

Rather than seeing procedures and documentation as passive background materials, I treat them as active structures that shape navigation, timing, interpretation, and judgment.

That means moving between design questions and conceptual questions: how systems can become more usable in practice, and how documents, investigations, and support tools shape what becomes visible, legible, and actionable in organizational life.

human-centered systems → procedural cognition → workflow visibility → decision support
// how I work
Close to real work, not abstracted away from it.

I combine document analysis, structured qualitative observation, usability testing, and lightweight computational analysis using Python and Streamlit — building prototypes that remain compliance-safe and external to validated systems.

Across six projects and five papers in progress, I keep returning to one underlying question: how do we make high-stakes procedural systems more legible for the people inside them?

research style
What my work is trying to do
// 01
Make friction visible

I study where documentation, workflow order, and interface structure create unnecessary interpretive burden — turning invisible friction into observable, designable problems.

// 02
Support judgment, not replace it

I care about tools and support layers that help people think more clearly — without pretending that complex regulated work can be fully automated away or that ambiguity is always a problem to solve.

// 03
Keep accountability in view

I want systems that make reasoning, interpretation, and responsibility more visible rather than prematurely compressed or hidden — especially as AI integration enters regulated investigation workflows.
