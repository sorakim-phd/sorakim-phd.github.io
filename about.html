About

I study how responsibility becomes structured, stabilized, and accelerated in regulated sociotechnical systems. My work focuses on the ways documents, procedural infrastructures, investigative routines, and computational representations shape accountability in organizational life.

orientation

What I keep returning to

I am drawn to organizational settings that look orderly on paper but remain interpretively fragile in practice. The core question running through my work is how responsibility becomes made, made legible, and sometimes made to appear settled too early.

grounding

Where the questions come from

My background is in pharmaceutical quality systems, where documentation, verification, deviations, CAPA/RCA investigations, and audit-facing records are part of everyday work. That environment shaped my interest in responsibility as a system property, not just an individual outcome.

Research program

My current research program follows responsibility across the organizational lifecycle: before action, during procedural execution, after investigation, and under computational mediation. Rather than treating accountability as a fixed category, I approach it as something produced through infrastructures of documentation, interpretation, and organizational closure.

This arc currently takes shape through four linked concepts: Anticipated Accountability Convergence (AAC), Cognitive Friction in Procedural Texts (CF), Responsibility Convergence (RC), and Premature Accountability Convergence (PAC).

AAC → CF → RC → PAC

How I think

I like research that treats documents, procedures, and systems as active organizational actors rather than passive background materials. I am especially interested in the moment when an explanation becomes administratively usable — when ambiguity narrows, responsibility hardens, and a system starts acting as though the question has already been resolved.

Methodologically, this means I move between conceptual framing, procedural text analysis, and close reading of document-based evidence, while keeping an eye on how interfaces and representations quietly reorganize judgment.
