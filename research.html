Research Program

Systems shape what gets seen —
and how decisions are made.

I study how documentation systems, review workflows, and decision-support environments shape human judgment in high-stakes regulated settings — where people follow procedures they do not fully understand, or make decisions under documentation they cannot easily navigate. My main orientation is HCI, grounded in STS theory that treats regulatory systems not just as compliance infrastructure, but as sociotechnical design problems.

Research identity

Designing for judgment under constraint.

I am interested in how people navigate procedural environments where formal rules and actual reasoning diverge — and how design can reduce that gap without breaking compliance constraints.

HCI

Human-centered systems and design for complex procedural work in regulated environments.

High-stakes

Pharmaceutical QC environments where judgment, traceability, and accountability all matter.

STS grounding

STS theory explains why design problems in regulated environments are structurally produced.

How I position the work
HCI at the center, STS as grounding.

My projects ask HCI questions about documents, workflows, support tools, and reasoning environments. STS conceptual work on accountability and procedural cognition explains why those systems produce the problems they do.

Research focus
Main agenda and supporting lenses
Main agenda
Human-centered systems for high-stakes procedural work

My research centers on how documentation, workflows, and support tools shape judgment in environments where people must work carefully, defensibly, and under strong formal constraints. I approach these as HCI problems: usability, interpretation, visibility, and reasoning support.

core orientation: designing systems that support judgment without flattening complexity
Supporting lens
Regulatory UX and procedural cognition

I study how people interpret instructions, manage ambiguity, and navigate cognitively demanding documentation in pharmaceutical quality control and other compliance-constrained environments.

Supporting lens
Accountability, responsibility, and closure

I use STS conceptual work on accountability and investigative closure to understand what support systems reveal, compress, or prematurely stabilize in regulated work.

Core questions
What this research asks
Question 01
How do procedural systems shape judgment?

I ask how documentation structures, review formats, and workflow environments distribute interpretive burden across workers in regulated settings — and how that burden can be better supported.

Question 02
Where does cognitive friction emerge?

I study where people pause, loop back, hesitate, or reconstruct meaning when written procedure and actual reasoning diverge — making invisible navigation friction observable as behavioral data.

Question 03
How can support tools help without oversimplifying?

I explore compliance-safe support layers, reasoning scaffolds, and interface interventions that improve navigation and causal reasoning while preserving interpretive openness.

Question 04
What changes under AI mediation?

I investigate how AI systems may accelerate accountability convergence, compress interpretive space, and reshape responsibility in high-stakes procedural environments — and how design can counter that.

Methods
Qualitative, computational, and design-oriented
Qualitative
Document analysis, observation, and usability study

I use document analysis, qualitative coding, usability testing, and structured observation to understand how people work through regulated documentation, review, and investigative processes. Studies range from focused pilot observations to workplace usability evaluations, all with GMP-experienced practitioners.

Computational / design
Metadata analysis, visualization, and interaction prototyping

I use Python and Streamlit to build lightweight interaction prototypes, analyze workflow metadata, visualize navigation patterns, and construct behavioral tracking tools — all designed to remain compliance-safe and external to validated systems.

STS foundations
Theoretical grounding for the HCI agenda
Four STS conceptual frameworks that explain why the design problems I study are structurally produced — showing why accountability, procedural cognition, and investigative closure operate the way they do in regulated systems, and what that means for design.
AAC
Anticipated Accountability Convergence

How accountability expectations become aligned before work begins — through procedural rules, documentation regimes, and evaluative infrastructures that preconfigure what counts as defensible conduct.

before action · interpretive expectations
CF
Cognitive Friction

How procedural text structure creates localized judgment demands during execution — grounding the SOP Accessibility and Cognitive Navigation projects. Empirical pilot: 500 procedurally realistic sentences.

during execution · procedural cognition
RC
Responsibility Convergence

Why investigations often stabilize responsibility through administratively legible outcomes — grounding the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder and Premature Causal Convergence projects.

after events · investigative closure
PAC
Premature Accountability Convergence

How AI-mediated systems may accelerate convergence before ambiguity has been meaningfully examined — directly grounding the AI extension of the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder as a counter-PAC design.

AI mediation · decision closure
Paper directions
Five HCI papers in progress
paper 01
document usability accessibility
SOP Accessibility and Compliance-Constrained Design
HCI paper · project-linked · SOP Retrieval System (N=20)

A paper on document accessibility and compliance-constrained design in pharmaceutical quality control. Argument: access failure is a judgment failure. Design contribution: usability improvements can emerge through external support layers without modifying validated source files.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
paper 02
reasoning support investigation design
Reasoning Under Closure Pressure: Scaffolding Root Cause Analysis in Regulated Environments
HCI paper · two-project pair · RCA Scaffolder + Premature Causal Convergence (N=20 each)

Combines a pilot study of premature causal convergence (N=20) with a usability evaluation of a scaffold designed to counter it (N=20). Initial single-cause reasoning shifted toward expanded multi-factor explanations over time (20/20). 80% of scaffold participants showed increased causal breadth.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
paper 03
document navigation traceability
Document Navigation as a Design Problem: Traceability and Behavioral Friction in GMP Environments
HCI paper · two-project pair · Cross-Reference + Cognitive Navigation (N=20 each)

Combines a metadata-driven cross-reference system (N=20, 30–60% faster task completion) with a behavioral navigation tracking prototype (N=20, 20/20 loopback). Together: where a practitioner loops back is a signal the document structure failed, not the user.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
paper 04
workflow UX observability
Workflow Friction and Observability in Deviation–CAPA Review Cycles
HCI paper · project-linked · Smart Feedback & Review Dashboard (N=20)

A paper on review interaction, workflow friction, and observability in validated pharmaceutical quality review systems. Argument: observability is a precondition for coordination. N=20 — all participants expressed adoption intent after reviewing the prototype.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
paper 05 · umbrella
regulatory UX concept paper writing now
Regulatory UX: Toward a Human-Centered Framework for Compliance-Constrained Interaction
HCI concept paper · umbrella framework · connects all six projects

A concept paper introducing Regulatory UX as a new HCI research frame — the study of worker interaction experience inside compliance-constrained systems, where procedural documents, audit workflows, and investigation structures shape navigation, cognition, and decision-making. Positions the six-project portfolio as a coherent empirical program.
