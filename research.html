FDA document analysis

My current empirical project examines how responsibility becomes stabilized within regulatory investigation documents. Using FDA Warning Letters and selected Form 483 observations from drug manufacturing inspections, I analyze how regulatory agencies articulate responsibility, enforcement reasoning, and corrective action expectations in response to observed violations. The study traces patterns of responsibility convergence within real-world regulatory investigations.

These materials provide a rare public record of how responsibility, enforcement reasoning, and corrective action expectations are articulated in regulatory investigations. Using this corpus, I trace patterns of responsibility convergence in real-world document settings.

why this project: to show how accountability is made administratively legible through document infrastructures, not simply assigned after the fact

Method and analytic focus

  • Dataset: FDA Warning Letters (drug manufacturing inspections) with matched Form 483 observations when available
  • Unit of analysis: violation / observation blocks within inspection documents
  • Approach: qualitative document analysis combining close reading and structured coding
  • Coding focus: responsibility attribution, procedural framing, corrective action language, and convergence patterns in regulatory reasoning
empirical aim: ground the broader responsibility program in observable regulatory texts while extending the PAC framework through document-based evidence

Anticipated Accountability Convergence (AAC)

before action / interpretive expectations / prospective structuring

AAC theorizes accountability as a prospective structuring condition rather than a purely retrospective evaluative event. The framework explains how procedural structuring, documentation regimes, and evaluative anticipation converge to stabilize defensible interpretation before action unfolds.

  • Focus: how organizations preconfigure what will count as accountable conduct before execution.
  • Mechanism: procedural structuring + documentation regimes + evaluative anticipation.
  • Core argument: what converges is not behavior itself, but interpretive expectations of defensible action.
  • Extension: calibrated convergence as a curvilinear relationship between convergence intensity and organizational effectiveness.
before action: interpretive expectations become prospectively aligned
Full-paper manuscript in preparation for submission to the National Communication Association (NCA).

Cognitive Friction in Procedural Texts (CF)

during execution / procedural language / localized judgment demands

CF examines how procedural language structures localized judgment demands during execution. It conceptualizes cognitive friction as the co-occurrence of conditional branching, cross-references, exception structures, and discretionary qualifiers within procedural sentences.

  • Focus: how documentation distributes interpretive work and localizes responsibility during execution.
  • Empirical pilot: 500 procedurally realistic sentences drawn from public regulatory and safety materials.
  • Operationalization: rule-based annotation of structural features within sentence-level procedural text.
  • Core claim: responsibility-relevant judgment demands are embedded in document architecture, not only in human error or post-hoc evaluation.
during execution: sentence structure shapes interpretive burden and attributable judgment
Expanded empirical version in progress, including corpus enlargement, feature co-occurrence analysis, and annotation reliability testing.

Responsibility Convergence (RC)

after events / investigative closure / administratively legible attribution

RC explains why organizational investigations such as root cause analysis recurrently stabilize accountability at identifiable actors even when failures are distributed across sociotechnical systems. The paper specifies a structural convergence dynamic rather than a purely psychological bias account.

  • Focus: why investigative systems designed for systemic explanation still produce administratively legible accountability outcomes.
  • Mechanisms: procedural ambiguity, attributional closure pressure, and accountability legibility bias.
  • Core claim: accountability converges because organizations require interpretable and actionable attribution anchors under investigative pressure.
  • Contribution: reframes blame as a structural output of accountability infrastructures rather than a simple individual bias.
after events: causal ambiguity is translated into administratively actionable responsibility
PAC AI mediation temporal accountability

Premature Accountability Convergence (PAC)

under AI mediation / representational compression / temporal contraction

PAC analyzes how AI systems reshape the timing and scope of responsibility stabilization in organizational settings. Rather than treating AI as an autonomous decision-maker, the paper conceptualizes computational systems as representational accelerators within existing accountability infrastructures.

  • Focus: when and how responsibility converges too early under computational mediation.
  • Mechanisms: representational compression, epistemic authority amplification, and temporal contraction of sensemaking.
  • Core claim: AI may not determine who is responsible, but it can alter how quickly and how narrowly responsibility becomes actionable.
  • Contribution: shifts attention from algorithmic bias toward the temporal architecture of accountability.
under AI mediation: convergence can accelerate before ambiguity has been meaningfully examined
Abstract in preparation for submission to the Society for Social Studies of Science (4S).
