Core research questions

from documents to decision support
question 01

How do procedural systems shape judgment?

I ask how documentation structures, review formats, and workflow environments distribute interpretive burden across workers in regulated settings.

question 02

Where does cognitive friction emerge?

I study where people pause, loop back, hesitate, or reconstruct meaning when written procedure and actual reasoning diverge.

question 03

How can support tools help without flattening complexity?

I explore support layers, scaffolds, and interface interventions that improve navigation and reasoning while preserving interpretive openness.

question 04

What changes under AI mediation?

I investigate how AI systems may accelerate closure, compress ambiguity, and reshape accountability in high-stakes procedural environments.

Current empirical work

public document analysis in progress

FDA document analysis

My current empirical project examines how responsibility becomes stabilized within regulatory investigation documents. Using FDA Warning Letters and selected Form 483 observations from drug manufacturing inspections, I analyze how agencies articulate responsibility, enforcement reasoning, and corrective action expectations in response to observed violations.

This project gives me a public document corpus for studying how procedural systems translate ambiguity into actionable decisions and how responsibility becomes legible in real-world regulatory settings.

current role in the program: empirical grounding for questions about reasoning, closure, and accountability in high-stakes document systems

Method and analytic focus

  • Dataset: FDA Warning Letters with matched Form 483 observations when available
  • Unit of analysis: violation or observation blocks within inspection documents
  • Approach: qualitative document analysis combining close reading and structured coding
  • Focus: attribution language, procedural framing, corrective action expectations, and convergence patterns in regulatory reasoning
broader aim: connecting HCI questions about judgment and support with public evidence from real regulatory systems
