Research Program

A research agenda on worker support in regulated systems.

I study how documentation systems, review workflows, and reasoning environments shape judgment, coordination, and accountability in high-stakes regulated systems.

Research identity

Designing better support under constraint.

My core question is not whether regulation should be relaxed. It is how demanding, rule-bound work can be made more navigable for the people inside it — without collapsing judgment into automation or breaking formal constraints.

HCI

Human-centered systems for high-stakes, rule-bound work.

High-stakes

Pharmaceutical QC as the empirical site where judgment, traceability, and accountability are tightly coupled.

STS grounding

STS and HCI together: STS explains formal accountability; HCI tests design interventions on the ground.

How I position the work
HCI at the center, STS as grounding.

The research program starts from a simple observation from QC practice: when regulated work becomes difficult, the outcome is often framed as an individual problem even when the underlying conditions are structural.

What this site shows
Six projects · five HCI papers · four STS foundations.

That is why the portfolio stays focused on work conditions and accountability. The HCI projects study mechanisms — navigation burden, premature closure, review opacity, traceability gaps. The STS papers explain why those mechanisms matter inside formal systems.

Scope

Research focus
Main agenda and supporting lenses
Main agenda
Human-centered systems for high-stakes procedural work

My research centers on how documentation, workflows, and support tools shape work in environments where people must act carefully under strong formal constraints. I approach these as HCI problems because interfaces and procedures help determine what becomes visible, actionable, and easy to justify.

core orientation: designing systems that support judgment without flattening complexity
Supporting lens
Regulatory UX and procedural cognition

I study how people interpret instructions, manage ambiguity, and navigate cognitively demanding documentation in pharmaceutical quality control and other compliance-constrained environments — especially where mistakes can later become audit or accountability issues.

Supporting lens
Accountability, responsibility, and closure

I use STS work on accountability, closure, and formal documentation to explain why some explanations settle quickly while others remain difficult to articulate inside formal systems.

Core questions
What this research asks
Question 01
How do procedural systems shape judgment?

I ask how documentation structures, review formats, and workflow environments distribute interpretive burden across workers in regulated settings — and how that burden can be better supported without weakening regulatory structure.

Question 02
Where does cognitive friction emerge?

I study where people pause, loop back, hesitate, or reconstruct meaning when written procedure and actual reasoning diverge — treating those moments as evidence that system structure, not only individual skill, is shaping the work.

Question 03
How can support tools help without oversimplifying?

I explore external support layers, reasoning scaffolds, and interface interventions that improve navigation and causal reasoning while preserving interpretive openness and human judgment.

Question 04
What changes under AI mediation?

I investigate how AI systems may compress interpretive space too early — and how AI can instead be constrained to reopen comparison, prompt evidence review, and preserve human judgment.

Methods
Qualitative, computational, and design-oriented
Qualitative
Document analysis, observation, and usability study

Methodologically, I combine document analysis, qualitative coding, usability testing, structured observation, and lightweight computational prototyping to study regulated work without altering validated systems.

Computational / design
Metadata analysis, visualization, and interaction prototyping

I use Python and Streamlit to build external prototypes, visualize navigation patterns, inspect workflow signals, and make otherwise hidden burden discussable as a design problem.

STS foundations
Theoretical grounding for the HCI agenda
Four STS conceptual frameworks that explain why the design problems I study are structurally produced — showing why accountability, procedural cognition, and investigative closure operate the way they do in regulated systems, and what that means for design.
AAC
Anticipated Accountability Convergence

One STS strand asks how accountability expectations are set up before action through procedural rules, documentation regimes, and evaluative infrastructures that preconfigure what counts as acceptable conduct.

before action · interpretive expectations
CF
Cognitive Friction

A second strand examines how procedural text structure creates localized judgment demands during execution — grounding the SOP Accessibility and Cognitive Navigation projects.

during execution · procedural cognition
RC
Responsibility Convergence

A third strand examines why investigations often settle around fast, documentable explanations and identifiable actors — grounding the Premature Causal Convergence and RCA Scaffolder projects.

after events · investigative closure
PAC
Premature Accountability Convergence

A fourth strand asks how AI-mediated systems might intensify or counter that convergence — grounding the AI extension of the RCA Scaffolder as a judgment-preserving design argument.

AI mediation · decision closure

A paper on document accessibility and compliance-constrained design in pharmaceutical quality control. Argument: retrieval burden shapes interpretation well before later judgment. Design contribution: external support layers can reduce access friction without modifying validated documents.

HCI paper · two-project pair · RCA Scaffolder + Premature Causal Convergence

Combines a pilot study of premature causal convergence with a usability evaluation of a scaffold designed to counter it. Argument: broader reasoning may need to be supported before investigations settle too quickly on one explanation.
