🧩 Background

human burden of compliance

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, a deviation occurs whenever an operation diverges from an approved procedure. Each deviation must be formally investigated and connected to a CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) plan intended to prevent recurrence.

These processes are essential for product safety and regulatory compliance, but they also create substantial cognitive and administrative burden for document owners and reviewers. In many regulated environments, deviation and CAPA records are maintained within validated Word-based templates, meaning their structure cannot be casually redesigned without triggering re-validation.

As a result, workflow friction accumulates inside a documentation system that is structurally rigid but socially variable: the format is fixed, yet the review experience still depends heavily on reviewer style, comment habits, and interpretation differences.

⚠️ Observation

real review frictions

Observed pain points

  • Different reviewers applied different feedback standards and writing styles.
  • Batch numbers, deviation IDs, and analyst names had to be repeatedly copied across linked records.
  • Even small clarifications could trigger multi-day review loops.
  • Repeated revisions increased fatigue and reduced confidence for document owners.

Why this mattered

These patterns did not reflect individual failure. They reflected a structural interaction problem: people had to compensate for rigid documentation systems that offered little support for consistency, visibility, or efficient review coordination.

💬 In regulated environments, safety protocols protect the product — but not always the people maintaining them.

🎯 Design question

HCI framing

How might review clarity, feedback consistency, and turnaround time be improved while maintaining full compliance within validated documentation systems?

↔ Workflow change illustration

before / after

Before

  • Reviewer expectations remained tacit rather than visible.
  • Comments arrived in inconsistent formats and levels of detail.
  • Document owners often revised reactively, not strategically.
  • Revision loops accumulated without a clear sense of bottleneck location.
  • Turnaround time depended on reviewer-specific styles.

After

  • Recurring comment types are clustered into clearer review categories.
  • A checklist layer makes feedback expectations more legible.
  • Revision patterns can be tracked across stages and reviewers.
  • Loop-heavy zones become visible through timestamp-based workflow mapping.
  • The system shifts from opaque review interaction to more observable review logic.
This project reframes review delay not as a matter of individual slowness, but as a workflow legibility problem within a constrained documentation system.

🧠 Method

workflow analysis + low-code support

1. Review interaction mapping (Excel)

Constructed a structured map of the deviation → investigation → CAPA review cycle using shared identifiers such as deviation ID, batch number, document owner, reviewer role, and revision timestamp.

2. Reviewer checklist prototype

Developed a standardized checklist derived from recurring historical review comments. Similar issues were grouped and translated into a more consistent review schema.

3. Feedback latency heatmap

Used revision timestamps to visualize high-density revision zones, slow review periods, and recurring bottleneck points. Excel handled first-pass workflow mapping, while Python can extend the model into reviewer-by-reviewer latency distributions.

4. Analyst usability survey

Designed a short Likert-scale survey to capture analyst perceptions of feedback clarity, review fairness, workload, and turnaround expectations within documentation review cycles.

💻 Computational extension

python depth

What Python adds

  • turnaround-time distributions by reviewer or stage
  • revision loop frequency analysis
  • bottleneck detection across document stages
  • comment clustering and repeated-friction patterns

Why it matters

Python makes the workflow argument more rigorous. Instead of only saying that review felt inconsistent, it allows the project to show where delays accumulate, which stages create more loops, and how friction patterns repeat across the lifecycle of controlled documents.

🧪 Evaluation

prototype + user perception

Evaluation setup

Because validated templates could not be redesigned directly, the intervention remained external to the live controlled system. The interaction map, checklist prototype, and latency views were treated as workflow-support artifacts rather than system replacements.

review artifacts survey workflow insight

What was assessed

  • perceived clarity of reviewer feedback
  • consistency of review expectations
  • confidence during revision cycles
  • perceived fairness of turnaround logic

Why this matters

The project does not claim that formal review authority should be removed. Instead, it asks a more HCI-relevant question: can workflow-support artifacts make review interaction more legible, less repetitive, and less cognitively draining without disrupting compliance?

📄 Paper in progress

research output

This project is being developed into a paper on workflow friction, feedback loops, and human-centered review support in regulated documentation systems. The paper extends the case study from practical workflow pain points into a broader HCI argument about how review infrastructures create hidden burdens through opacity, inconsistency, and repeated loopbacks.

More broadly, the project asks how human-centered design can operate in environments where the formal structure of documentation is difficult to change, yet the interactional burden of review remains substantial.

💬 Reflection

why it is HCI

This project treats review not as a neutral administrative step, but as an interaction system. The central issue is not simply whether feedback exists, but how that feedback is structured, interpreted, and translated into repeated work.

From an HCI perspective, the important insight is that workflow friction can emerge even when everyone is acting correctly. Review loops, inconsistent expectations, and hidden bottlenecks are interaction problems embedded in procedural systems.

In that sense, the project expands my research from document accessibility into workflow legibility: not just helping users find the right document, but helping them understand where they are in a multi-stage review process and why work keeps looping back.
