🧩 Background

human burden of compliance

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, a Deviation occurs whenever an operation diverges from an approved procedure — even a brief delay or temperature fluctuation. Each deviation must be formally investigated and connected to a CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) plan that prevents recurrence.

While these processes are essential for product safety and regulatory compliance, they also generate substantial cognitive and administrative burden for analysts and QA reviewers. In many GMP environments, deviation and CAPA documents are stored as validated Word templates. Because these templates are validated, their structure cannot be modified without system re-validation.

As a result, workflow design is effectively locked, and review inefficiencies accumulate inside compliance constraints. This creates a gap between regulatory rigor and human sustainability in documentation work.

⚠️ Observation

real review frictions

Observed pain points

  • Different QA reviewers applied different feedback standards and writing styles.
  • Batch numbers, deviation IDs, and analyst names had to be repeatedly copied across documents.
  • Even small clarifications could trigger multi-day review loops.
  • Repeated revisions increased fatigue and reduced confidence for document owners.

Why this mattered

These patterns did not reflect individual failure. They reflected a structural interaction problem: human reviewers had to compensate for rigid documentation systems that offered little support for consistency, visibility, or efficient review coordination.

💬 In regulated environments, safety protocols protect the product — but not always the people maintaining them.

🎯 Design question

HCI framing

How might we improve review clarity, feedback consistency, and turnaround time while maintaining full compliance within validated GMP documentation systems?

🧠 Method

workflow analysis + low-code support

1. Review interaction mapping (Excel)

Constructed a structured mapping of the deviation → investigation → CAPA review cycle using shared identifiers such as deviation ID, batch number, analyst ID, reviewer ID, and revision timestamp.

2. Reviewer checklist prototype

Developed a standardized QA checklist derived from recurring historical review comments. Similar issues were clustered and translated into a more consistent review schema.

3. Feedback latency heatmap

Used revision timestamps to visualize high-density revision zones, slow review periods, and recurring bottleneck points. Excel handled the first-pass mapping, while Python can extend this into reviewer-by-reviewer latency distributions.

4. Analyst usability survey

Conducted a short Likert-scale survey to capture analyst perceptions of feedback clarity, review fairness, workload, and turnaround expectations.

💻 Computational extension

python depth

What Python adds

  • turnaround-time distributions by reviewer
  • revision loop frequency analysis
  • bottleneck detection across document stages
  • comment clustering and repeated-friction patterns

Why it matters

Python makes the workflow argument more rigorous. Instead of only saying that review felt inconsistent, it allows the project to show where delays accumulate, which reviewers or stages create more loops, and how friction patterns repeat across the documentation lifecycle.

🧪 Evaluation

prototype + user perception

Evaluation setup

Because validated templates could not be redesigned directly, the intervention remained external to the live controlled system. The review interaction map, checklist prototype, and heatmap views were treated as workflow-support artifacts rather than system replacements.
