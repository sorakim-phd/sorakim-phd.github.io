SOP Accessibility System

A compliance-safe search and retrieval layer for scanned procedural archives. This project examines how document accessibility shapes navigation, interpretive effort, and procedural confidence in regulated environments, treating retrieval friction as an HCI problem rather than a purely administrative inconvenience.

// 01
Background
problem framing

In regulated and procedure-heavy environments, formal operating documents are not just references. They shape how work is found, interpreted, executed, and later reviewed. They function as part of the infrastructure through which high-stakes work becomes legible, repeatable, and auditable.

In practice, however, procedural archives are not always easy to use. Digital records are often stored as scanned PDFs, making them difficult to search, cross-reference, or navigate efficiently. Users may rely on file titles, folder memory, and manual scrolling simply to locate the correct procedure.

That burden does not end at retrieval. When documentation is difficult to access or interpret, hesitation, workaround behavior, and procedural mismatch can later appear as if they were individual performance problems rather than features of the documentation system itself. Accessibility therefore matters not only for convenience, but for how judgment and accountability are organized under constraint.

In regulated systems, documentation does not merely store instructions. It shapes how work is found, understood, and later judged.
// 02
Observation
document friction
// observed pain points
  • Scanned procedural files blocked or weakened keyword search.
  • Document titles were often too broad, too similar, or too generic.
  • Users opened multiple incorrect files before locating the right one.
  • Access depended heavily on familiarity with archive naming conventions.
  • Time-sensitive situations amplified navigation burden and uncertainty.
// what this suggested

The problem was not simply missing search functionality. Retrieval difficulty emerged from a deeper mismatch between how procedural archives were organized and how users actually searched under constraint. This suggested that accessibility problems could systematically introduce hesitation, misnavigation, and interpretive variance before any formal task error occurred.

Before / After
system change
// before
  • Title-based browsing across scanned files
  • Keyword search unavailable or unreliable
  • Users manually opened multiple documents
  • Relevant sections found through scrolling and memory
  • Similar titles increased retrieval uncertainty
// after
  • Keyword-based entry into the document archive
  • Ranked results with snippets and highlighted matches
  • Document identifiers and page cues surfaced early
  • No-result states handled explicitly
  • External support layer added without modifying source files
// 04
Method
prototype + mixed methods
01
metadata and keyword mapping

Reviewed approximately 500 procedural documents and extracted representative keywords, document identifiers, page references, and related process categories to build a searchable metadata index.

02
structure and overlap analysis

Used Excel and Python to inspect overlapping terms, recurring categories, and retrieval-relevant keyword patterns. This helped identify where users were likely to face ambiguity: similar titles, overlapping terminology, and mismatches between document-level labels and section-level content.

03
prototype artifact

Developed an external retrieval aid — a prototype accessibility layer that allowed users to search by keywords, document cues, and related terminology without altering validated source files.

04
usability testing

Conducted an initial usability study with likely end users, using the prototype both as a design intervention and as a way to observe how documentation access shapes confidence, search behavior, and navigation experience.

The prototype functioned not only as a design intervention, but also as an analytic probe for studying where retrieval succeeds, where it fails, and how document structure shapes interpretation under constraint.

// 06
AI Support Layer
reasoning support

The second version extends the prototype into a lightweight AI-oriented support layer. It does not replace document reading or provide compliance decisions. Instead, it explains why results appeared, surfaces related procedures, and adds context-preserving support notes during navigation.

Open AI Support Layer ↗
💬Explains why a result appeared, making retrieval logic visible rather than implicit
📎Surfaces related procedures and neighboring documents that may bear on the same task
💡Adds context-support notes to preserve interpretive openness during navigation
🛡️Designed as support, not automation — no compliance answers, no black-box decisions
// design principle

Support, not autopilot. The layer expands visibility into documentation and keeps interpretive options visible rather than collapsing them too early.

// future direction

Currently rule-based and lightweight. Future versions could incorporate LLMs for context-sensitive support while preserving human decision visibility and accountability.

AI here is treated as part of a responsibility-sensitive design strategy: it expands visibility into documents and reasoning, rather than substituting for human judgment.

Usability Study
initial evaluation · N=12

An initial usability survey was completed by 12 participants. Most reported regular SOP use and frequent search behavior in their work. Ratings were high for ease of use, usefulness of result cues, and overall intuitiveness, suggesting that a lightweight external layer can improve retrieval without requiring changes to validated source files.

// what worked
  • Users could enter through task-relevant keywords rather than file-name memory alone.
  • Snippets and highlighting reduced early-stage uncertainty.
  • Document identifiers and page cues improved search confidence.
// remaining friction
  • No-result queries still created uncertainty about whether the issue was query wording or archive coverage.
  • Highly similar document titles could continue to blur differentiation even after retrieval improved.
  • These cases suggested that accessibility problems persist not only at the interface layer, but also in metadata design and archive structure.
The strongest signal was not simply that search became easier, but that ambiguity became more visible. The prototype helped separate user difficulty from structural difficulty in the archive itself.
// 08
What This Project Revealed
analytic takeaway

This project suggests that document accessibility is not a neutral layer sitting outside the work itself. It can act as a hidden condition in how judgment unfolds under constraint. Retrieval difficulty shapes what people notice first, how long they hesitate, which document they trust, and how confidently they proceed.

That matters analytically because system-level friction can later be misrecognized as an individual problem. What appears downstream as inconsistency, hesitation, or deviation may sometimes begin upstream as a structural issue in how documentation is organized, labeled, or made searchable.

retrieval difficulty → interpretive variance → judgment under constraint → perceived responsibility
// 09
Connection to Research Program
documents → judgment → responsibility

This project begins with document retrieval, but its broader significance lies in what retrieval difficulty reveals. When documentation is hard to search, fragmented, or cognitively demanding, the burden travels downstream into hesitation, workaround behavior, variable interpretation, and later judgments about whether someone followed procedure correctly.

SOP accessibility is therefore not separate from accountability. It is one of the infrastructural conditions under which accountability is stabilized, interpreted, and sometimes unfairly individualized. This makes document usability a meaningful part of the larger research agenda on cognitive friction, responsibility, and reasoning support in regulated systems.

documentation structure → cognitive friction → judgment under constraint → responsibility

Paper in Progress
research output

This project is being developed into a paper on accessibility, document usability, and compliance-constrained design in regulated environments. The central argument is that accessibility improvements in validation-sensitive systems can often be achieved through external support layers rather than direct modification of the underlying source system.

More broadly, the paper positions retrieval friction as an analytic entry point into how documentation structure shapes cognitive friction, interpretive variance, downstream judgment, and responsibility in high-constraint work settings, with later extensions toward AI-mediated interfaces that support procedural reasoning while preserving human accountability.
