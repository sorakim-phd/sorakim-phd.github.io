SOP Accessibility and Compliance-Constrained Design
HCI paper · project-linked · SOP Accessibility

This paper argues that document inaccessibility in regulated environments produces systematic judgment errors, not merely compliance gaps.

Evaluated in task-based retrieval experiments (N=12), showing measurable retrieval delays and navigation behavior changes.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → project

Examines document accessibility, information retrievability, and compliance-constrained design in pharmaceutical QC environments — developed from the SOP Accessibility project (N=12, 4.83/5, 100% adoption intent).

  • Argument: access failure is a judgment failure — workers who cannot retrieve the right SOP cannot apply it correctly.
  • Design contribution: usability improvements can emerge through external support layers without modifying validated source files.
  • Positions document accessibility as a substantive HCI problem in regulated environments.
// paper_02
reasoning support investigation design
Reasoning Under Closure Pressure: Scaffolding Root Cause Analysis in Regulated Environments
HCI paper · two-study paper · RCA + PCC

This paper shows that premature causal convergence is a structural condition — and that scaffold design can counter it.

Pilot observation (N=5) + scaffold usability evaluation (N=15) in pharmaceutical investigation workflows.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → RCA → PCC

Combines two studies: a pilot observation of premature causal convergence in unassisted investigation reasoning (N=5), and a usability evaluation of a scaffold designed to counter it (N=15).

  • Pilot: 5/5 began with a single cause — revised toward systemic explanations only when given unstructured time.
  • Scaffold: 80% of participants increased causal breadth; all initially individual-blame hypotheses revised toward systemic explanations.
  • Argument: investigation quality is a cognitive systems design problem, not a training problem.
// paper_03
document navigation traceability
Document Navigation as a Design Problem: Traceability and Behavioral Friction in GMP Environments
HCI paper · two-study paper · Cross-Ref + Navigation

This paper argues that practitioner loopbacks signal document-structure failure, not individual error.

Cross-reference system (N=17) + behavioral navigation tracking prototype (N=5) in GMP documentation environments.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → cross-ref → navigation

Combines two studies: a metadata-driven cross-reference system addressing structural traceability gaps (N=17), and a behavioral navigation tracking prototype making invisible navigation friction observable (N=5).

  • Cross-ref: 30–60% faster task completion · 2× document discovery · path completion 30–50% → 70–90%.
  • Navigation: 5/5 loopback observed · less experienced users identified SOP structure — not the interface — as friction source.

// paper_05
regulatory UX concept paper
Regulatory UX: Toward a Human-Centered Framework for Compliance-Constrained Interaction
HCI concept paper · umbrella framework · connects all six projects

This paper proposes Regulatory UX as a framework for analyzing and redesigning how compliance-constrained systems shape navigation, reasoning, and accountability.

Conceptual synthesis connecting all six projects into a unified design argument about regulated documentation as interaction environments.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗

Introduces Regulatory UX as a new HCI research frame — the study of worker interaction experience inside compliance-constrained systems, where procedural documents, audit workflows, and investigation structures actively shape navigation, cognition, and decision-making.

  • Core argument: regulated documentation systems are not neutral containers — they are active interaction environments that shape how workers search, reason, assign blame, and close investigations.
  • Framework contribution: Regulatory UX names and structures a design space where usability, compliance, and accountability intersect.
  • Connects all six projects as a coherent empirical program instantiating the Regulatory UX argument across multiple failure modes.

Theoretical grounding

The following papers develop the theoretical foundations of the program, focusing on how accountability and judgment are structured in complex systems.

These manuscripts provide the theoretical infrastructure behind the HCI papers — drawing on science and technology studies (STS) to explain how accountability, procedural cognition, and investigative closure operate in regulated sociotechnical systems. They explain why the design problems the projects address are structurally produced, not accidental.

Anticipated Accountability Convergence
STS conceptual manuscript

This paper argues that accountability expectations are prospectively aligned before action, shaping what counts as defensible conduct.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ PDF ↗

Develops a structural theory of how accountability expectations become aligned before action through the convergence of procedural rules, documentation regimes, and evaluative infrastructures.

  • Focus: how organizations preconfigure what counts as accountable conduct before execution begins.
  • HCI relevance: explains why design interventions must account for prospective accountability structures — not just usability at the moment of use.

Responsibility Convergence in Organizational Investigations
STS conceptual manuscript

This paper explains why organizational investigations recurrently stabilize blame at individuals despite distributed system failures.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ PDF ↗

Explains why organizational investigations like root cause analysis recurrently stabilize accountability at identifiable actors even when failures are distributed across sociotechnical systems.

  • Core mechanisms: procedural ambiguity, attributional closure pressure, and accountability legibility bias.
  • HCI relevance: grounds the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder — explains the structural conditions the scaffold is designed to counter.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN

Analyzes how AI-mediated representations reshape the timing and scope of responsibility stabilization by compressing interpretive space and accelerating convergence within accountability infrastructures.
