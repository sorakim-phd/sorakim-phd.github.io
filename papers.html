paper 01 document usability accessibility

SOP Accessibility and Compliance-Constrained Design

HCI paper · project-linked

A paper on document accessibility, information retrievability, and compliance-constrained design in pharmaceutical quality control environments — developed from the SOP Accessibility project (N=12, 4.83/5, 100% adoption).

  • Argument: access failure is a judgment failure — workers who cannot find the right standard operating procedure (SOP) at the right moment cannot apply it correctly.
  • Design contribution: usability improvements can emerge through external support layers without modifying validated source files, establishing a template for compliance-safe HCI intervention.
  • Broader claim: positions document accessibility as a substantive HCI problem in regulated environments, not merely an administrative inconvenience.
Paper direction active · N=12 usability data collected
paper 02 reasoning support investigation design

Reasoning Under Closure Pressure: Scaffolding Root Cause Analysis in Regulated Environments

HCI paper · two-project pair

A paper combining two complementary studies: a pilot observation of premature causal convergence in unassisted investigation reasoning (N=5), and a usability study of a human-centered scaffold designed to counter it (N=15). Together, they establish the problem empirically and test a design response.

  • Pilot finding: all 5 practitioners began deviation analysis with a single cause — and revised toward systemic explanations only when given 10 minutes of unstructured thinking time. Closure pressure truncates this naturally.
  • Scaffold finding: N=15. 80% increased causal breadth. All 5 who began with individual blame revised toward systemic factors after using the scaffold.
  • Design argument: investigation quality is a cognitive systems design problem. Lightweight scaffolds can protect reasoning space without altering validated documentation structures.
  • Theoretical grounding: connects to the PAC (Premature Accountability Convergence) framework — positioning the scaffold as a counter-PAC design.
Paper direction active · N=5 pilot + N=15 usability data collected
paper 03 document navigation traceability

Document Navigation as a Design Problem: Traceability and Behavioral Friction in GMP Environments

HCI paper · two-project pair

A paper combining two complementary projects: a metadata-driven cross-reference system addressing structural traceability gaps (N=17), and a behavioral navigation tracking prototype that makes invisible navigation friction visible (N=5). Together, they argue that GMP document navigation is a design problem — not a user competence problem.

  • Cross-reference finding: N=17. Task completion time reduced 30–60%. Documents discovered per session increased 2–3×. Path completion rate up from 30–50% to 70–90%.
  • Navigation finding: N=5. All participants exhibited loopback behavior. Less experienced practitioners identified SOP structure — not the interface — as the source of navigation difficulty.
  • Design argument: where a practitioner loops back is not a mistake — it is a signal that the document structure failed to resolve a judgment demand the first time.
Paper direction active · N=17 + N=5 usability data collected
paper 04 workflow UX observability

Workflow Friction and Observability in Deviation–CAPA Review Cycles

HCI paper · project-linked

A paper on review interaction, workflow friction, and observability in validated pharmaceutical quality review systems — developed from the Smart Feedback and Review Dashboard prototype (N=6).

  • Problem: quality review cycles (deviation reports → QA review → corrective and preventive action closure) are opaque — workload imbalance and delay accumulation are invisible until they have already become a problem.
  • Design argument: validated documentation systems still produce interaction problems that can be studied and redesigned around. Observability is a precondition for coordination.
  • Finding: N=6 (5 GMP-experienced, 1 non-GMP). All participants expressed adoption intent after reviewing the prototype interface — indicating perceived fit with real workflow needs.
Paper direction active · N=6 prototype feedback collected
paper 05 regulatory UX concept paper writing now

Regulatory UX: Toward a Human-Centered Framework for Compliance-Constrained Interaction

HCI concept paper · umbrella framework

A concept paper introducing Regulatory UX as a new HCI research frame — the study of worker interaction experience inside compliance-constrained systems, where procedural documents, audit workflows, and investigation structures actively shape navigation, cognition, and decision-making.

  • Core argument: regulated documentation systems are not neutral containers for compliance information. They are active interaction environments that shape how workers search, reason, assign blame, and close investigations.
  • Framework contribution: Regulatory UX names and structures a design space that existing HCI frameworks have not systematically addressed — the space where usability, compliance, and accountability intersect.
  • Connects all six projects: positions the project portfolio as a coherent empirical program instantiating the Regulatory UX argument across multiple failure modes.
Currently writing · umbrella paper for the full research program
STS foundations · 4 manuscripts

Theoretical grounding

depth behind the HCI work

These four manuscripts provide the theoretical infrastructure behind the HCI papers — drawing on science and technology studies (STS) to explain how accountability, procedural cognition, and investigative closure operate in regulated sociotechnical systems. They are not separate from the HCI agenda. They explain why the design problems the projects address are structurally produced, not accidental.

These manuscripts treat regulatory systems as sociotechnical phenomena — where accountability is organizationally produced, cognition is shaped by document architecture, and AI integration can accelerate or disrupt responsibility stabilization. Reading them alongside the HCI papers shows why design interventions in regulated environments must engage with accountability structures, not just usability metrics.
AAC anticipatory alignment organizational infrastructures

Anticipated Accountability Convergence

STS conceptual manuscript

PDF

Develops a structural theory of how accountability expectations become aligned before action through the convergence of procedural rules, documentation regimes, and evaluative infrastructures.

  • Focus: how organizations preconfigure what counts as accountable conduct before execution begins.
  • Mechanism: procedural structuring + documentation regimes + evaluative anticipation.
  • HCI relevance: explains why design interventions in regulated environments must account for prospective accountability structures — not just usability at the moment of use.
before action → interpretive expectations are prospectively aligned
CF procedural texts document architecture

Documenting Judgment: Cognitive Friction and Responsibility in Procedural Documentation

STS conceptual + empirical manuscript

PDF

Examines how procedural language structures localized judgment demands during execution and how document architecture distributes interpretive burden while preserving individual accountability.

  • Empirical pilot: 500 procedurally realistic sentences derived from public regulatory and safety documentation.
  • Operationalization: conditional branching, cross-references, exception structures, and discretionary qualifiers within procedural sentence architecture.
  • HCI relevance: grounds the SOP Accessibility and Cognitive Navigation projects — explains why document structure produces friction that interface redesign alone cannot solve.
during execution → sentence structure shapes interpretive burden and attributable judgment

RC investigations closure pressure

Responsibility Convergence in Organizational Investigations

STS conceptual manuscript

PDF

Explains why organizational investigations such as root cause analysis recurrently stabilize accountability at identifiable actors even when failures are distributed across sociotechnical systems.

  • Core mechanisms: procedural ambiguity, attributional closure pressure, and accountability legibility bias.
  • Focus: why system-oriented investigations still generate actor-centered accountability outcomes.
  • HCI relevance: grounds the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder and Premature Causal Convergence projects — explains the structural conditions the scaffold is designed to counter.
after events → causal ambiguity is translated into administratively actionable responsibility
PAC AI mediation temporal accountability

Premature Accountability Convergence

STS conceptual manuscript

PDF

Analyzes how AI-mediated representations reshape the timing and scope of responsibility stabilization by compressing interpretive space and accelerating convergence within accountability infrastructures.

  • Mechanisms: representational compression, epistemic authority amplification, and temporal contraction of sensemaking.
  • Focus: not only who becomes responsible, but how quickly accountability stabilizes — and what AI does to that timeline.
  • HCI relevance: directly grounds the AI extension of the RCA Reasoning Scaffolder — the scaffold is explicitly designed as a counter-PAC intervention.
under AI mediation → convergence may accelerate before ambiguity has been meaningfully examined
