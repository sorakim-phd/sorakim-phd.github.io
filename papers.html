Papers & Manuscripts

Papers & Manuscripts

These papers develop a unified research program on how regulated systems shape judgment, responsibility, and accountability—bridging empirical HCI studies with STS foundations.

What's here

One research program, two layers.

The STS manuscripts develop the conceptual account of accountability, closure, and formal systems. The HCI papers test related questions empirically through prototypes and observations in pharmaceutical quality environments. Together they ask how judgment and responsibility are shaped from within regulation rather than outside it.

HCI papers
Five empirical & design papers

Each HCI paper starts from a concrete friction in QC practice: retrieval burden, navigation burden, review opacity, or premature investigative closure. The empirical question is not only whether a tool is usable, but what kind of work problem it helps make visible.

STS foundations
Four theoretical foundations

The STS layer asks why those frictions matter in the first place: how accountability is organized, narrowed, and increasingly mediated by AI within formal systems.

SOP Accessibility and Compliance-Constrained Design
HCI paper · project-linked · Procedural Visibility in SOP Navigation

Interpretive burden is produced at the point where retrieval and interpretation become structurally decoupled in regulated work.

Shows that retrieval delays systematically shift effort from navigation to interpretation, increasing uncertainty before formal decision points. Connects the SOP accessibility prototype to a broader argument about compliance-constrained design.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → Project PDF ↗

Examines document accessibility, information retrievability, and compliance-constrained design in pharmaceutical QC environments — developed from the Procedural Visibility in SOP Navigation project and its pilot evaluation.

  • Argument: access failure can translate into judgment difficulty — when workers cannot retrieve the right SOP, application becomes more error-prone.
  • Design contribution: usability improvements can emerge through external support layers without modifying validated source files.
  • Positions document accessibility as a substantive HCI problem in regulated environments.

Reasoning Under Closure Pressure: Scaffolding Root Cause Analysis in Regulated Environments
HCI paper · two-study paper · Expanding Causal Reasoning under Closure Pressure + Premature Convergence under Closure Pressure

Premature causal convergence is not simply a reasoning failure, but a condition produced by investigation structure under closure pressure.

Pairs an empirical baseline of premature convergence with a scaffolded intervention that preserves comparison longer before accountability narrows. Extends the observational pilot into a design response to closure pressure.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → RCA → PCC PDF ↗

Combines two studies: a pilot observation of premature causal convergence in unassisted investigation reasoning, and a usability evaluation of a scaffold designed to counter it.

  • Pilot: initial single-cause reasoning often shifted toward broader multi-factor explanations over time.
  • Scaffold: many participants showed increased causal breadth, and several moved from person-centered explanations toward broader systemic ones.
  • Argument: investigation quality is partly a cognitive systems design problem, not only a training problem.
paper 03
document navigationtraceability
Document Navigation as a Design Problem: Traceability and Behavioral Friction in GMP Environments
HCI paper · two-study paper · Cross-Reference Burden and Traceability Visibility + Navigation Friction and Loopback Behavior

Loopbacks and broken trace paths are structural signals of documentation burden, not merely traces of inefficient searching.

Combines cross-reference visibility with behavioral tracking to show how navigation patterns reveal hidden structural burden. Builds from traceability failure toward a behavioral account of document friction.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → Cross-ref → Navigation PDF ↗

Combines two studies: a metadata-driven cross-reference system addressing structural traceability gaps, and a behavioral navigation tracking prototype making invisible navigation friction observable.

  • Cross-reference: task completion improved, document discovery increased, and path completion became more reliable during pilot evaluation.
  • Navigation: consistent loopback patterns observed across participants · less experienced users identified SOP structure — not the interface — as the friction source.
paper 04

Workflow Friction and Observability in Deviation–CAPA Review Cycles
HCI paper · project-linked · Making Review Friction Visible in Regulated Work

Coordination failure is not only a communication problem, but a consequence of review burden remaining structurally unobservable.

Shows how a visibility layer can externalize delay accumulation, comment burden, and review-loop formation before coordination breaks down. Extends the navigation papers into a workflow observability argument.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ → Project PDF ↗

Examines review interaction, workflow friction, and observability in validated pharmaceutical quality review systems — developed from the Making Review Friction Visible in Regulated Work prototype.

  • Problem: quality review cycles are opaque — workload imbalance and delay accumulation are invisible until they have already become a problem.
  • Participants consistently expressed adoption interest after reviewing the prototype interface.
paper 05 · umbrella

Regulatory UX: Toward a Human-Centered Framework for Compliance-Constrained Interaction
HCI concept paper · umbrella framework · connects all six projects

Regulatory UX names a design space in which usability, compliance, and accountability are not separate constraints, but co-produced design conditions.

Synthesizes the six HCI projects into one design argument: regulated documentation systems are active interaction environments that shape search, reasoning, coordination, and closure. Connects the empirical papers into a single HCI research program.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ PDF ↗

Introduces Regulatory UX as an HCI research frame for worker interaction inside compliance-constrained systems, where procedural documents, audit workflows, and investigation structures shape navigation, cognition, and decision-making.

  • Core argument: regulated documentation systems are not neutral containers — they are active interaction environments that shape how workers search, reason, and close investigations.
  • Framework contribution: Regulatory UX names and structures a design space where usability, compliance, and accountability intersect.
  • Connects all six projects as a coherent empirical program instantiating the Regulatory UX argument across multiple failure modes.

Theoretical grounding
Theoretical infrastructure behind the HCI papers.
These manuscripts draw on science and technology studies (STS) to explain how accountability, procedural cognition, and investigative closure operate in regulated sociotechnical systems — showing why the design problems the projects address are structurally produced, not accidental.

Anticipated Accountability Convergence
STS conceptual manuscript

Accountability expectations are often set up before action through procedures, documentation, and evaluation rules.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ PDF ↗

Develops a structural theory of how accountability expectations become aligned before action through the convergence of procedural rules, documentation regimes, and evaluative infrastructures.

  • Focus: how organizations preconfigure what counts as acceptable and accountable conduct before execution begins.
  • HCI relevance: explains why design interventions must account for prospective accountability structures — not just usability at the moment of use.

STS conceptual manuscript

Organizational investigations often settle on individual responsibility even when failures are more distributed.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN

Explains why organizational investigations like root cause analysis often settle on identifiable actors even when failures are distributed across sociotechnical systems.

AI mediation can compress interpretive space too early, affecting how quickly responsibility settles.

Preprint · 2026 · SSRN
SSRN ↗ PDF ↗

Analyzes how AI-mediated representations reshape the timing and scope of accountability by compressing interpretive space and accelerating convergence within formal decision systems.

  • Mechanisms: representational compression, epistemic authority amplification, temporal contraction of sensemaking.
