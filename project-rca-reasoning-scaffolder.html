RCA Reasoning Scaffolder

case study · investigation reasoning · regulated systems

A human-centered investigation support project exploring how structured reasoning scaffolds can improve root cause analysis (RCA) in GMP deviation investigations.

Background

In GMP environments, deviations require structured investigations to identify root causes and determine corrective and preventive actions (CAPA). These investigations are usually documented through Root Cause Analysis (RCA).

However, RCA reasoning often unfolds under time pressure and strict documentation formats. Investigators must connect multiple contributing conditions — procedures, equipment states, environmental factors, and operator actions — while producing a linear explanation suitable for regulatory documentation.

This creates a tension between how reasoning actually unfolds and how investigation narratives must be written.

Observation

  • Investigations frequently converge on a single explanation early in the reasoning process.
  • Alternative causal hypotheses are harder to maintain once documentation becomes linear.
  • Causal reasoning is largely internal and difficult to externalize.
  • Complex contributing factors may disappear during narrative compression.

Design Question

How can investigation reasoning be supported without modifying validated GMP documentation systems?

Method

  • Mapped typical RCA reasoning structures using investigation templates and 5-Whys logic.
  • Designed a lightweight reasoning scaffold prompting investigators to consider alternative hypotheses.
  • Introduced simple causal diagrams to externalize reasoning pathways.
  • Conducted qualitative evaluation of whether the scaffold helped structure investigation thinking.

Results & Expected Outcomes

Dimension Traditional RCA With Scaffold
Investigation reasoning Linear narrative Structured exploration
Hypothesis diversity Often limited Broader causal consideration
Cognitive load High Reduced via visual reasoning support
Compliance risk None None

📄 Paper in progress

research output

This project can also be developed into a paper on cognitive-pathway modeling, procedural mismatch, and human reasoning under regulatory constraint.

The broader paper contribution would be that procedural systems are written as linear sequences, while human reasoning unfolds as adaptive path structures.

In paper form, the project argues that the gap between written procedure and actual cognitive navigation is measurable — and that this gap should be treated as a design problem rather than merely a user problem.

Reflection

This project reframes investigation quality as a cognitive systems design issue rather than purely an individual skill problem.

By supporting structured exploration of causal pathways, investigators can maintain broader reasoning during deviation analysis without compromising regulatory documentation requirements.
