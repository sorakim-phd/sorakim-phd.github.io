🧩 Background

problem framing

In Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) environments, deviations require structured investigations to identify root causes and determine corrective and preventive actions. These investigations are typically documented through Root Cause Analysis (RCA) — a formal process connecting the observed event to its underlying causes and informing CAPA planning.

In practice, investigators must connect multiple contributing conditions — procedures, equipment states, environmental factors, and operator actions — while producing a clear written explanation suitable for quality review and regulatory documentation.

This creates a tension between how reasoning actually unfolds and how investigation narratives are expected to be written.

💬 Human reasoning is often exploratory and branching, while procedural documentation expects a linear and stable explanation.

⚠️ Observation

reasoning friction

Observed pain points

  • Investigations frequently converged on a single explanation early in the reasoning process.
  • Alternative causal hypotheses became harder to maintain once documentation turned linear.
  • Causal reasoning was largely internal and difficult to externalize.
  • Complex contributing factors could disappear during narrative compression.

Why this was difficult

In a regulated GMP environment, investigation quality depends not only on domain knowledge but also on how well reasoning can be structured under documentation pressure. The challenge was not "change the official RCA system," but rather "support better reasoning without touching validated documentation workflows."

🎯 Design question

HCI framing

How can a lightweight reasoning scaffold help investigators preserve broader causal exploration in GMP deviation analysis while remaining compatible with validated documentation practices?

🧠 Method

low-code + qualitative

1. RCA structure mapping

Mapped typical RCA reasoning structures using common investigation templates and 5-Whys logic to identify where explanation paths tend to narrow too quickly.

2. Lightweight scaffold design

Designed a reasoning scaffold that prompted investigators to consider alternative hypotheses and parallel contributing conditions before settling on a final explanatory path.

3. Causal diagram support

Introduced simple causal diagrams to externalize intermediate reasoning steps and make branching pathways more visible during investigation work.

4. Prototype-based usability study

Conducted a structured usability study with 15 GMP-experienced participants, measuring changes in reasoning breadth, interface usability, and accountability attribution before and after scaffold use.

🖥 Prototype

UI + AI extension

The prototype is a step-by-step reasoning scaffold that guides investigators through hypothesis generation, factor review, and evidence attachment before committing to a final explanation. The AI extension adds reasoning-space expansion — surfacing alternative causal pathways and flagging premature accountability convergence.

🧪 Usability study

N = 15 · GMP-experienced participants

Evaluation setup

Participants read a standardised GMP deviation scenario and recorded their initial causal assessment. They then used the scaffold prototype and completed a structured survey measuring usability, reasoning change, and accountability attribution. All 15 participants had GMP or quality systems experience; 53% reported frequent RCA experience.

pre-use assessmentscaffold usepost-use survey

What was measured

  • change in number of causes considered (pre vs. post)
  • whether new causes were surfaced that were not initially considered
  • whether the scaffold pushed toward individual blame attribution
  • whether multiple explanations remained visible
  • ease of use, interface clarity, and real-world adoption intent

🔑 Key finding: reasoning breadth increased

Before using the scaffold, 80% of participants believed the deviation had only one possible cause. After using the scaffold, 80% reported an increase in the number of causes they were considering — and 80% said they had thought of new causes they had not previously considered. The three participants who reported no change were those with the most extensive GMP investigation experience.

💬 Qualitative feedback

The most common improvement suggestion was that the concept of a "reasoning scaffold" itself was unfamiliar — participants noted that GMP environments typically prioritize rapid closure, and that the tool's value would benefit from a brief conceptual introduction. This finding points toward adoption barriers being conceptual rather than interactional: the interface itself was clear, but the idea that investigation reasoning deserves structured support was new.

🔥 Accountability finding

PAC counter-evidence

One of the central concerns in my broader research program is premature accountability convergence (PAC) — the tendency for regulated investigation systems to narrow responsibility toward individual actors before systemic factors have been fully examined. The usability study provides direct evidence on this point.

Q11 — Individual blame pressure

15/15 participants strongly disagreed that the scaffold pushed them toward attributing the cause to a specific individual or action.

Q12 — Multiple explanations

15/15 participants agreed that the scaffold actively helped them maintain multiple possible explanations rather than converging too quickly.

Q13 — Closure speed

15/15 participants strongly disagreed that responsibility felt fixed more quickly than in a typical RCA process.

This is not only a usability result — it is empirical evidence that a lightweight reasoning scaffold can resist premature accountability convergence in regulated investigation environments.

📄 Paper in progress

research output

This project is being developed into a paper on cognitive-pathway modeling, procedural mismatch, and human reasoning under regulatory constraint. The paper extends the case study into a broader HCI argument about how investigation reasoning unfolds under documentation pressure in regulated environments.

The broader contribution is that procedural systems are written as linear sequences, while human reasoning unfolds as adaptive path structures. The gap between written procedure and actual cognitive navigation can therefore be studied and treated as a design problem rather than merely a user problem.

💬 Reflection

why it is HCI

This project reframes investigation quality as a cognitive systems design issue rather than purely an individual skill problem. In other words, weak investigations are not always the result of weak investigators; they can also emerge from reasoning environments that encourage premature linearization.

From an HCI perspective, the important insight is that supporting structured exploration of causal pathways can improve investigation reasoning without compromising regulatory documentation requirements. The usability study further showed that the main adoption barrier is conceptual rather than interactional — suggesting that the next design challenge is not interface clarity, but how to make the value of reasoning support legible to users who have been trained to close investigations quickly.
